# DUK — Pre-rating context briefing (2026-06-09)

Prepared for AI Thesis re-evaluation of Layer 1 power names. Session lens: how DIRECTLY does DUK own the AI power bottleneck (disclosed datacenter load share + rate structures capturing growth), vs a generic "load growth" narrative. Regulated-return capture is the central question.

---

## Pre-research mental model

Note: the local DUK thesis.md is an unfilled template and news-log.md is empty, so this mental model is from training knowledge only, not prior project research. Duke is a fully regulated utility (DEC/DEP in the Carolinas, plus Florida, Indiana, Ohio, Kentucky) with one of the strongest Southeast datacenter pipelines — the Carolinas host major Apple/Google/Meta campuses historically, and I recalled a multi-GW datacenter ESA pipeline being built up through 2025 alongside a large (~$85-90B) five-year capital plan and 5-7% EPS growth guidance. I expected the most recent quarter I knew (Q4 2025 / early 2026) to show datacenter ESAs in the mid-single-digit GW range and an NC rate case in motion. I did NOT have the Q1 2026 numbers or the new datacenter tariff proposal.

## Findings (sources)

1. **Q1 2026 beat, driven by datacenter/load narrative.** Adjusted EPS $1.93 vs $1.86 consensus (+3.8%); revenue $9.18B vs $8.49B est (+8.1%); Electric Utilities & Infrastructure adjusted segment income $1.404B vs $1.276B prior-year. 2026 guidance $6.55-$6.80. (Investing.com Q1 2026 slides recap, 2026-05; earnings call 2026-05-05, Motley Fool transcript.)
2. **7.6 GW of executed datacenter ESAs; 15.4 GW late-stage pipeline.** +2.7 GW signed since the Q4 call; "nearly two-thirds already under construction"; additional 7.8 GW "high-confidence, late-stage pipeline" (15.4 GW total inclusive of signed ESAs). First power deliveries "as early as the second half of 2027 and into 2028," ramping to full contracted load "through the early 2030s" (Brian Savoy, Q1 2026 call, 2026-05-05; Utility Dive 2026-05). No hyperscaler named on the call.
3. **$103B 2026-2030 capital plan — largest fully regulated plan in the industry.** Up 18% from $87B (2025-2029). Drives ~9.6% earnings-base (rate base) growth through 2030; ~65% of spend is grid + generation; ~14 GW new generation by 2031 incl. 4.5 GW batteries from late 2027; $3.2B Anderson gas plant in service end-2030; up to $3.1B IRA tax-credit monetization through 2028. 5-7% LT EPS growth through 2030, "top half of the range beginning in 2028" (Harry Sideris). (Q4 2025 8-K / Investing.com slides recap 2026-02; Fortune 2026-04-25; Utility Dive.)
4. **Contract safeguards + proposed mandatory datacenter tariff.** Signed ESAs carry "minimum demand provisions, credit support, refundable capital advances and termination charges" (Sideris, Q1 2026 call). Separately, in the Nov 2025 NC rate case Duke proposed a mandatory tariff for ≥50 MW customers: minimum monthly bills tied to ≥80% of requested load, exit fees, and curtailment hours — effective Jan 1, 2027 if approved (DCD; WUNC/WFAE 2026-06-08; Nelson Mullins blog). These protect existing ratepayers; they do NOT give Duke premium pricing.
5. **NC rate case pending — allowed ROE is the cap on upside.** DEC/DEP filed general rate cases 2025-11-20 requesting 10.95% ROE (90th percentile of recent requests); current approved ROEs are 10.1% (DEC-NC) and 9.8% (DEP-NC) from the 2023 cases. NCUC decision expected late 2026, rates effective 2027-01-01. NC AG is contesting (~$1.4B); consumer advocates pushed for separate datacenter rate class at hearings 2026-06-08. (EQ Research ROE comparison 2026-02-12; NCDOJ; WUNC 2026-06-08; duke-energy.com rate case page.)
6. **Customer identity:** trade press places Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta, Apple and the OpenAI-affiliated Stargate project among announced/committed NC datacenter campuses (Business North Carolina; Fortune 2026-04-25), but Duke itself has not attributed any signed ESA to a named hyperscaler in 2026 disclosures found.

## DIFF (vs pre-research mental model)

- **Bigger and faster than I had it:** 7.6 GW signed (I assumed mid-single-digit) and a 15.4 GW late-stage pipeline; capital plan is $103B, not ~$87B — raised 18% in Feb 2026.
- **New since my model:** the proposed mandatory ≥50 MW datacenter tariff (min-take 80%, exit fees) filed with the Nov 2025 rate case; consumer-advocate pushback as of 2026-06-08 (yesterday) is an active regulatory risk/feature.
- **Timing reality check:** essentially none of the contracted datacenter GW generates revenue today — first deliveries H2 2027. The Q1 2026 beat is current-business + weather/volumes, not AI revenue.
- **Local files were empty** — thesis.md is a blank template; this briefing is the first substantive DUK research artifact in the repo.

## Evidence for D1 (% of revenue datacenter-tied TODAY)

- **No disclosed figure.** Neither the Q1 2026 call nor the Q4 2025 release quantifies datacenter share of current revenue or load. FLAG: gap, do not guess.
- **Directional math:** the 7.6 GW of executed ESAs begin taking power "as early as H2 2027" (Savoy, Q1 2026 call) — i.e., contracted AI datacenter revenue today ≈ $0 from these agreements. Legacy datacenter load exists (Apple/Google/Meta NC campuses operating since the 2010s) but is not broken out anywhere found.
- Implication for D1: datacenter-tied revenue TODAY is small and undisclosed; the exposure is forward rate-base growth, not current revenue mix.

## Evidence for D2 (regulated return capture vs pricing power)

- Upside is structurally capped at allowed ROE on rate base: 10.1% DEC-NC and 9.8% DEP-NC currently approved (2023 cases, per EQ Research 2026-02-12); 10.95% requested in the pending case, decision late 2026, rates effective 2027-01-01.
- Growth flows through rate base, not price: $103B capex → ~9.6% earnings-base growth → 5-7% EPS CAGR through 2030 (Q4 2025 8-K; Q1 2026 call). That is the entire transmission mechanism — no merchant/premium pricing on datacenter power.
- The proposed datacenter tariff (min monthly bills at ≥80% of requested load, exit fees, curtailment) is ratepayer-protection and capex de-risking, not margin capture. ESA safeguards (minimum demand, credit support, refundable capital advances, termination charges) de-risk the $103B plan but earn the same allowed ROE.
- Active headwinds on capture: NC AG contesting ~$1.4B of the rate request; advocates pushing a separate (likely lower-margin/stricter) datacenter rate class (WUNC 2026-06-08); Feb 2026 NC appeals-court ruling found a prior fuel-rate approval violated law (no refunds) — a reminder of regulatory friction.
- Contrast for the re-rating: vs a merchant generator (CEG/VST/TLN-style premium PPAs), DUK converts the same demand into regulated-ROE rate-base growth — slower, capped, but contracted and lower-variance.

## Evidence for D5 (named hyperscaler commitments / contracted GW / signed agreements)

- **Contracted GW: strong and specific.** 7.6 GW executed ESAs (2026-05-05 call), ~2/3 under construction; 15.4 GW late-stage pipeline incl. signed; +2.7 GW signed in a single quarter.
- **Named commitments: none from Duke.** No hyperscaler named in the Q1 2026 transcript or found 2026 releases. Trade press attributes NC campuses to Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Meta, Apple, and OpenAI-affiliate Stargate (Business North Carolina; Fortune 2026-04-25), but these are county/state announcements, not Duke-attributed ESAs.
- Terms disclosed in aggregate only (minimum demand, termination charges, capital advances); no per-customer GW, tenor, or pricing disclosed.

## FLAGS

1. D1 has no disclosed number — datacenter % of current revenue/load is a hard gap; rating must rely on the H2 2027 revenue-start fact.
2. Hyperscaler names come from trade press, not Duke disclosure — treat D5 "named commitments" as unverified at the issuer level.
3. NC rate case (decision late 2026) is the swing variable for both allowed ROE (10.95% asked vs 9.8-10.1% current) and the datacenter tariff design; consumer-advocate intervention is live as of 2026-06-08.
4. Could not verify the 7.6 GW / 15.4 GW figures against the Q1 2026 10-Q directly in this pass — sourced from the earnings call transcript and Utility Dive/Investing.com recaps.
