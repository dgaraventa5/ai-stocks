## 6. The AI-vs-aerospace thesis split (the question Dom asked)

**How much of this is genuinely "AI" vs aerospace/satcom?** Honest decomposition by FY2025 revenue:
- **~17% explicitly AI** (the xAI segment: Grok/X/AI compute, $3.2B). This is real, reported, GAAP-segment AI revenue — but it is **money-losing** ($6.8B segment cash burn) and largely the legacy xAI/X business, much of which (X social media) is **not AI-infrastructure** in the sense this project tracks.
- **~61% Starlink connectivity** — satcom, not AI. AI *optimizes* the network (Grok support, autonomy) but the revenue is broadband subscriptions, not AI.
- **~22% launch/Space** — aerospace. The AI link is indirect (manufacturing autonomy; and SpaceX rockets are the *enabler* of orbital data centers — a genuine and differentiated AI-infrastructure angle, but prospective).

**Net:** Dom's "AI-forward like Tesla" thesis is **defensible and materially stronger than I assumed pre-research** — because xAI is already consolidated and because the orbital-compute + gigawatt-cluster strategy is a real, vertically-integrated AI-infrastructure play (rockets → satellites → power → compute → models). **But:** (a) AI is today ~17% of revenue and deeply unprofitable; (b) the most compelling AI-infrastructure pieces (space data centers, V3 edge AI) are **roadmap, not revenue**; (c) much of the "AI revenue" is actually X/social. So the rubric's **D1 (AI revenue %)** lands around **3** today (10–25% band), with the bull case being **optionality**, not current mix. This is the auto-vs-AI analogy exactly: like Tesla, the AI is real and strategically central but is *embedded in / subsidized by* a hardware cash engine (Starlink here, autos there) and is mostly a forward call option in the valuation.

Public Class A holders have minimal governance rights. Extreme key-man dependence + controlled-company status. Source: S-1 risk factors / governance section.

The Space segment is heavily US-government-dependent (NASA/DoD). Starlink diversifies the *company* (consumer/enterprise/international, no single customer dominant in Connectivity), so company-wide single-customer concentration is moderate, but **government dependence in the Space segment is high and politically/contract-exposed** (and Musk-political-risk-adjacent). Source: S-1.
- **Balance sheet:** Strong and improving — ~net-cash pre-IPO, ~$75B raised. But sustained **mega-capex and net losses** mean the company is funding-dependent if AI/Starship burn outruns Starlink cash generation. Post-IPO this is well-covered; pre-profit-at-the-consolidated-level is the watch item.
- **Regulatory / dilution:** (a) **Cursor all-stock deal** = $60B of new Class A dilution at close (Q3 2026), priced on a 7-day VWAP — material share issuance + antitrust review (note the $4B antitrust break fee = non-trivial regulatory risk perceived by the parties). (b) Launch-license / FAA / FCC spectrum (Starlink) / orbital-debris regulatory exposure. (c) Standard newly-public litigation/disclosure risk with no track record. Source: 8-K 2026-06-16; S-1.
- **Thesis-break signals to watch:** Starlink ARPU decline accelerating without offsetting sub growth; AI-segment burn widening without a revenue path; Starship program slippage; Cursor deal breaking (break fees); any Musk-attention/political shock.
