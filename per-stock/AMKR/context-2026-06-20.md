## 1. Pre-research mental model (Step 1 — articulated BEFORE pulling data)

> Amkor is the #2 global OSAT (outsourced semiconductor assembly & test), behind ASE Technology. Back-end contract manufacturer: takes finished wafers from fabless/IDM customers and does packaging + final test. Historically a thin-margin, cyclical, capital-intensive business with heavy customer concentration in Apple (smartphone packaging). The AI angle is advanced packaging — fan-out (HDFO) and 2.5D platforms used for AI accelerators and HPC — but I believe the bulk of Amkor's revenue is still mainstream mobile/communications packaging, not AI. Most recent quarter per my training: somewhere in 2024, low-to-mid-single-digit growth, gross margin in the low-to-mid teens. I expect Apple is ~25-30% of revenue. I'm aware of an Arizona advanced-packaging facility tied to the TSMC/Apple Arizona ecosystem but unsure of its status (announced? under construction? operating?).

**The diff this exercise found (see §5):** the AI-packaging ramp is materially further along than my model assumed — advanced products are 81-83% of sales and AI/data-center is now the explicit growth driver with management guiding the AI packaging portfolio to "nearly triple in 2026." The stock has re-rated hard (all-time highs, P/S at 100th percentile of its 3-yr range) — it is no longer the sleepy cyclical my model held. The Arizona facility is under construction (groundbreaking Oct 2025), production not until early 2028 — so it is a future option, not a current earnings driver. Apple confirmed at 29.8%. Founder Kim family controls ~49.4% — a governance fact my model didn't hold.

## 6. Implications for ratings (flagging only — ratings proposed separately in §7 of return value)

- **D1 AI Revenue %:** Tricky. Advanced products = 81-83% of sales, but "advanced packaging" ≠ "AI-infrastructure revenue" — most advanced packaging is still flip-chip/WLP for smartphones (communications = 44-46% of total). The genuinely AI-tied slice is the data-center portion of Computing (Computing = 20-21% of total, of which data-center is a growing sub-share) plus the HDFO/2.5D AI-accelerator/CPU work. Best estimate: AI-infra-tied revenue is roughly **10-25%** of total and rising fast — points to D1 = 3 (not 4, because communications/mobile is still the plurality). Show the math; do not count all "advanced" as AI.
- **D2 Supply-chain position / D3 Moat:** #2 OSAT with real scale and advanced-packaging IP (HDFO, 2.5D), but mid-chain service provider with a thin ~14% gross margin and a powerful in-housing threat (TSMC 3DFabric) plus Chinese OSAT share gains. Pricing power is limited (materials pass-through, customer concentration gives Apple/Qualcomm leverage). Leans D2 ≈ 3, D3 ≈ 2-3.
- **D4 Capacity expansion:** Strong — $7B Arizona buildout (broken ground, customer-anchored, CHIPS-supported) + advanced-packaging capacity tripling in 2026. Clear 4-5.
- **D5 Hyperscaler exposure:** Indirect. Amkor's named large customers are Apple and Qualcomm, not hyperscalers; AI exposure flows through merchant accelerator/CPU vendors (e.g., AMD/others) and Apple silicon, not direct hyperscaler contracts. Leans D5 ≈ 2-3.
- **M1 EPS revisions:** Strongly positive (Needham/MS/B.Riley raises; FY26 EPS $1.74→$2.11). 4-5.
- **M2 Relative strength:** All-time highs, big outperformance, +8.25% single day. 5 — but note the rule-14 expectations flag (this strength IS the crowding risk).
- **M3 Insider activity:** Routine post-retirement trim by ex-CEO; no buying; Kim-family secondary is an overhang but estate/diversification-flavored. Default ~3.
- **R1 Customer concentration:** Apple 29.8% → squarely band 3 (top customer 25-40%). Real but not existential.
- **R2 Geographic:** Asset leg (Korea/China/Taiwan plants) + China-for-China + Taiwan-strait + demand leg → this is a **multi-leg** name; the point-of-billing table is misleading. Leans 2-3.
- **R3 Balance sheet:** Net cash (cash+STI ~$1.99B vs debt ~$1.46B; ND/EBITDA ≈ −0.2x); strong. But heavy capex ahead ($7B Arizona) will consume FCF. 4 (not 5 — the buildout is a real future call on liquidity).
- **R4 Regulatory/litigation:** No major disclosed litigation; CHIPS-incentive clawback conditions and export-control overhang are the watch items. ~4.
- **R5 Disruption risk:** Physical back-end manufacturing → defaults to **5** (non-Layer-10; AI is a tailwind, not a disruptor of the packaging service model). The TSMC-in-housing threat is a competitive/moat issue (D2/D3), NOT an R5 business-model-obsolescence issue.

## 7. Layer-placement assessment

**Proposed:** `04 Semiconductor Equipment & Materials / OSAT & advanced packaging` (new sub-layer).

**Assessment: the sub-layer makes sense, but flag the naming tension.** Amkor is a **back-end contract manufacturer / service provider**, not an equipment maker (it BUYS tools from AMAT/ASML/BESI/etc.) and not a pure materials supplier. Putting it under "Semiconductor Equipment & Materials" is therefore a slight category stretch — but it is the closest existing layer (it sits in the semi-manufacturing value chain, upstream of finished chips, alongside the equipment/materials that feed fabs and packaging houses). Creating the explicit "OSAT & advanced packaging" sub-layer correctly distinguishes the service-provider business model from the equipment names already in Layer 04.

**Alternatives considered:**
- A standalone "Layer 4.5 / Advanced Packaging & Test" or folding into Layer 05 (Fabs) — Amkor is conceptually adjacent to fabs (it's the step right after wafer fab). But OSAT is a distinct outsourced-service model from IDM/foundry fabs, so a dedicated sub-layer is cleaner than lumping with TSM/INTC fabs.
- **Recommendation:** keep it in Layer 04 under the new "OSAT & advanced packaging" sub-layer, but consider lightly relabeling the parent (e.g., "Semiconductor Equipment, Materials & Packaging") if/when more OSAT names (ASE/ASX, PTI, JCET) are added, so the service-provider model isn't mislabeled as "equipment." Companion candidates for the sub-layer if expanded: ASX (ASE), BESIY-adjacent is equipment not OSAT. Confirm with Dom.
