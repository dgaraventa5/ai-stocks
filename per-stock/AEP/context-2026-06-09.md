# AEP — Pre-rating context briefing (2026-06-09)

Prepared for AI Thesis re-evaluation of Layer 1 power names. Session lens: how DIRECTLY does AEP own the AI power bottleneck (disclosed datacenter load share + rate structures capturing growth) vs. a generic "load growth" narrative, and how much upside flows through given regulated allowed-ROE caps.

---

## Pre-research mental model

AEP is a fully regulated multi-state utility (11 states, ~5.6M customers) and arguably the most datacenter-exposed regulated name: AEP Ohio's data center tariff (PUCO-approved 2025) requires large DC customers to commit to ~85% of contracted load for ~12 years with exit fees, de-risking rate-base growth without creating merchant-style pricing upside. Prior belief: ~20+ GW of contracted incremental load through 2030 backed by financial commitments, commercial (DC-driven) load growing ~20% YoY. Most recent quarter known: Q1 2026 — op EPS $1.64 beat, 5-yr capex raised $6B to $78B, LT EPS CAGR target raised to >9% (news-log entry 2026-05-12).

## Findings (sources)

1. **Q1 2026 results (8-K Ex. 99.1, 2026-05-05, SEC EDGAR):** GAAP EPS $1.61 ($874M) vs $1.50 ($800M) Q1'25; operating EPS $1.64 ($891M) vs $1.54. Revenue $6,020M vs $5,463M (+10.2% YoY). 2026 op EPS guidance reaffirmed $6.15–$6.45. Op earnings CAGR >9% through 2030. Weighted avg basic shares 542M vs 533M (+1.7% YoY dilution).
   Source: https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/0000004904/000000490426000031/a1q20268kpressreleaseex991.htm

2. **Contracted load now 63 GW incremental by 2030 (8-K Ex. 99.1, 2026-05-05):** +7 GW signed in Q1 2026 (mostly Ohio and Texas), "backed by signed agreements with well-capitalized industrial customers, hyperscalers and data center developers." AEP Texas accounts for 41 GW of the 63. Per Q1 2026 earnings call: ~90% of the new load is data centers (incl. hyperscalers); 53 of 63 GW in Texas + Ohio; remaining 10 GW requires new generation.
   Sources: 8-K above; Q1 2026 call transcript (https://www.theglobeandmail.com/investing/markets/stocks/AEP/pressreleases/1714359/aep-q1-2026-earnings-transcript/)

3. **Pipeline trajectory:** 28 GW (Q3 2025) → 56 GW (Q4 2025, reported Feb 2026; AEP Texas 36 GW, PJM 15 GW, SPP 5 GW; ~90% data centers, >50% hyperscalers) → 63 GW (Q1 2026). Texas loads backed by "letters of agreement" under TX Senate Bill 6; other regions use letters of agreement or electric service agreements.
   Source: Utility Dive, 2026-02-13 (https://www.utilitydive.com/news/aep-large-load-data-center-earnings/812181/)

4. **Capital plan and earnings algorithm (8-K, 2026-05-05):** 5-yr plan raised $72B → $78B (2026–2030); ~11% annual rate-base growth; op EPS CAGR >9%; line of sight to >$10B additional (Piketon OH transmission, WY fuel cell, generation). Transmission = $33B (42% of plan). $2.6B follow-on equity priced at $127/share May 2026 (news-log 2026-05-12) funds part of this.

5. **Tariff/rate structure (Q1 2026 call + PUCO):** Data center tariffs approved in Ohio; large-load tariffs approved in Indiana, Kentucky, West Virginia; filings pending in Michigan, Oklahoma, Texas, Virginia. AEP Ohio tariff (PUCO order 2025-07-09): new DC customers >25 MW pay minimum demand charge on 85% of subscribed capacity whether used or not, 12-year term incl. 4-yr ramp, proof of financial viability, exit fees. Revenue protected "through minimum demand charges embedded directly within these binding take-or-pay contracts" (CFO, Q1 2026 call).
   Sources: https://www.powermag.com/regulator-approves-aep-ohios-landmark-data-center-tariff/ ; transcript above

6. **Where the DC money goes:** AEP forecasts "up to $16 billion in cost offsets for existing customers" over the life of the large-load contracts — i.e., the contracted DC revenue above incremental cost is explicitly allocated to holding down other customers' bills, not to shareholder margin (8-K + Q1 2026 call). Consolidated earned ROE 9.1% in 2025, targeted ~9.3% in 2026, ~9.5% by 2030 (Q1 2026 call/slides via Utility Dive Feb 2026). Recently approved allowed ROEs: 9.84% Ohio, 9.65% Arkansas, 9.75% West Virginia (Q1 2026 reporting, BigGo/Alphastreet summaries of company slides).

7. **Tariff filtering effect (Ohio Capital Journal, 2026-02-20):** After the Ohio tariff required upfront financial commitments, projected new DC demand in AEP Ohio's queue dropped from ~30,000 MW to ~5,700 MW — i.e., ~80% of the raw pipeline was speculative. AEP frames this as the tariff working. The Ohio Manufacturers' Association appeal of the tariff is pending at the Ohio Supreme Court (filed Nov 2025; active as of Feb 2026).
   Sources: https://ohiocapitaljournal.com/2026/02/20/aep-ohio-says-new-data-center-tariff-is-working-critics-arent-buying-it/ ; https://ohiocapitaljournal.com/2025/11/13/ohio-manufacturers-association-challenges-new-utility-billing-for-data-centers/

8. **Named customers (Q1 2026 call):** SB Energy 10 GW data center campus, Piketon, Ohio; Google multibillion-dollar development, Putnam County, WV (early stage); Amazon data center, northwest Louisiana. Separately, Meta (via affiliate) filed with PUCO to draw power from the idle Green Chapel substation (built for Intel New Albany) for a 3-year term, 2026-01-01 to 2028-12-31 (WOSU, 2025-11-26: https://www.wosu.org/2025-11-26/meta-wants-to-draw-power-for-unused-new-albany-electrical-substation-built-for-intel-project). New Albany cluster customers (Google since 2019, AWS, Microsoft, Meta) are existing AEP Ohio loads but AEP does not disclose per-customer revenue.

9. **Load-growth proxies (no direct DC revenue disclosure):** Weather-normalized commercial sales (the class containing data centers): +10.6% 2024, +12.3% Q1 2025, guided +23.9% FY2025, +19% and +16.1% the following two years; commercial expected to rise from 34% to 45% of total retail sales by 2027 (Utility Dive, May 2025: https://www.utilitydive.com/news/aep-data-center-earnings-load-fehrman/740182/ ; T&D World 2025). Q1 2026 commercial sales growth % was not found in the transcript or 8-K — gap flagged below.

10. **PJM bottleneck (Q1 2026 call):** CEO Fehrman on PJM interconnection: "If something is not done now, I expect we could still be having these same conversations in 10 years" — PJM "is not expediting the connection of generation to load"; AEP "assessing all options" including structural changes to market participation. Texas timing also "highly dependent on the needed generation to support the load, which is supplied by others" (8-K).

## DIFF (mental model vs. findings)

- **Pipeline far larger than prior:** I carried ~20+ GW; actual contracted incremental load is 63 GW by 2030 (tripled since Oct 2025: 28→56→63 GW). Materially bigger than remembered.
- **Texas, not Ohio, is now the center of gravity:** 41 of 63 GW is AEP Texas (ERCOT) — and AEP Texas is wires-only T&D; generation is supplied by others. My Ohio-tariff-centric model is stale.
- **Commitment quality is heterogeneous:** Texas GW are backed by SB6 "letters of agreement," not the 12-yr/85% take-or-pay ESAs I associated with the Ohio tariff. The strongest structure (Ohio tariff) covers the smaller share, and Ohio's post-commitment queue is only ~5.7 GW.
- **The $16B reframe:** I hadn't internalized that AEP explicitly markets the DC contract value as *cost offsets for existing customers*. This is direct evidence the economics above allowed ROE go to ratepayers — confirms the regulated-cap thesis rather than premium capture.
- **Ohio tariff legal risk is live:** OMA appeal pending at Ohio Supreme Court — not in my prior model.
- **EPS algorithm upgraded:** 7–9% growth → ">9%" CAGR through 2030 on ~11% rate-base growth, funded partly by equity ($2.6B follow-on; share count +1.7% YoY).

## Evidence for D1 (% of revenue datacenter-tied TODAY)

- **No direct disclosure.** Neither the Q1 2026 8-K nor the call discloses current data center MW served, DC revenue, or DC % of sales. FLAG: D1 must be estimated from class-level proxies.
- Proxies: commercial class (contains data centers) was 34% of retail sales pre-ramp, guided to 45% by 2027 (Utility Dive 2025-05); commercial sales growth +10.6% 2024, +12.3% Q1 2025, ~24% guided FY2025. Q1 2026 revenue +10.2% YoY ($6,020M vs $5,463M, 8-K 2026-05-05) — blends rate cases and load, cannot isolate DC.
- Directional math (flagged as estimate, not disclosure): if commercial is ~40% of retail sales today and data centers drove the majority of commercial growth since 2023, DC-tied retail sales are plausibly in the high-single-digit to low-teens % of revenue today — but this is inference. The 63 GW is FUTURE load (by 2030) vs. AEP's ~32 GW of owned/contracted generation capacity today (8-K boilerplate), which shows the scale of the ramp, not today's mix.

## Evidence for D2 (regulated return capture vs pricing power)

- Returns are capped at allowed ROE: recently approved 9.84% (OH), 9.65% (AR), 9.75% (WV); consolidated earned ROE 9.1% in 2025, ~9.3% expected 2026, ~9.5% by 2030 — AEP earns BELOW allowed today (Q1 2026 reporting; Utility Dive 2026-02-13).
- No premium pricing: tariffs are protective (85% minimum demand take-or-pay, 12-yr, exit fees) — they de-risk recovery and shift cost responsibility to DCs, they do not let AEP price above cost-of-service. AEP explicitly forecasts "up to $16B in cost offsets for existing customers" from these contracts (8-K 2026-05-05) — the surplus goes to ratepayers.
- Upside transmission mechanism: growth flows through rate base (~11%/yr on $78B plan → >9% EPS CAGR), partially diluted by equity funding ($2.6B follow-on May 2026; shares +1.7% YoY). This is the textbook regulated model: volume growth → capex → allowed ROE on bigger base. Compare: a merchant (VST/CEG/TLN) captures price; AEP captures volume×allowed-ROE.

## Evidence for D5 (named hyperscaler commitments, contracted GW, signed ESAs)

- 63 GW contracted incremental load by 2030, ~90% data centers, >50% hyperscalers; +7 GW signed Q1 2026; 53 GW in Texas+Ohio (8-K 2026-05-05; Q1 2026 call; Utility Dive 2026-02-13).
- Named: SB Energy (10 GW campus, Piketon OH), Google (Putnam County WV, early stage), Amazon (NW Louisiana) — Q1 2026 call. Meta/Green Chapel substation PUCO filing (3-yr, 2026–2028, New Albany) — WOSU 2025-11-26. Existing New Albany cluster (Google, AWS, Microsoft, Meta) already on AEP Ohio.
- Commitment instruments: Ohio = tariff-backed ESAs (85%/12-yr take-or-pay, exit fees, financial viability proof); Texas (41 GW, the bulk) = SB6 letters of agreement — softer than ESAs; PJM/SPP = mix of LOAs and ESAs (Utility Dive 2026-02-13). Hyperscaler names behind most of the 63 GW are NOT individually disclosed.

## FLAGS

1. **D1 gap:** No disclosure of current DC load served or DC % of revenue — any D1 number is class-level inference. Could not find Q1 2026 commercial sales growth % (transcript/8-K silent); the 24% figure is FY2025 guidance from May 2025.
2. **41 of 63 GW are Texas letters of agreement** under SB6, not take-or-pay ESAs; AEP Texas is wires-only and generation timing "is supplied by others." Pipeline quality ≠ Ohio-tariff quality.
3. **Ohio tariff under appeal** (OMA, Ohio Supreme Court, pending as of 2026-02-20). Adverse ruling would weaken the strongest contractual structure in the story.
4. **Ohio queue shrank 30 GW → ~5.7 GW** once financial commitments were required — evidence that raw "pipeline" GW figures (anyone's) are ~80% air; the 63 GW is post-commitment but instrument quality varies.
5. **$16B of contract value is earmarked for existing-customer offsets** — direct evidence the DC economics above allowed ROE accrue to ratepayers, not shareholders. Earned ROE (9.1%) currently sits below allowed (~9.65–9.84%).
