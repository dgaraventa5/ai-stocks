# TLN — Pre-rating context briefing (2026-06-09)

Prepared for AI Thesis rating re-evaluation. Session lens: how DIRECTLY does TLN own the AI power bottleneck (signed hyperscaler PPAs at premium prices vs. commodity-price second-derivative exposure)?

---

## Pre-research mental model

TLN is the most direct pure-play on AI-datacenter power among the IPPs: it operates the ~2.5 GW Susquehanna nuclear plant (90% owned, 10% Allegheny Electric Cooperative) and signed the landmark AWS deal — originally a behind-the-meter campus sale (~$650M, March 2024), restructured in June 2025 into a front-of-the-meter PPA for up to 1,920 MW through 2042 after FERC rejected the behind-the-meter ISA amendment in November 2024. The most recent quarter I know of is Q1 2026 (reported May 5): adj. EBITDA $473M (>2x YoY), adj. FCF $350M, 2026 guidance reaffirmed, AWS FTM transmission reconfiguration executed during the spring Susquehanna refueling outage, and the Cornerstone acquisition (~2.45 GW gas in IN/OH) expected to close summer 2026. Versus peers: CEG has the larger nuclear fleet and the Microsoft/Crane restart, VST has Comanche Peak plus a gas fleet and growing datacenter pipeline; TLN is smaller but carries the highest single-contract hyperscaler exposure relative to its size, with ~35% of gross margin contracted long-term and a 50% target. Open questions going in: how much AWS revenue is actually flowing TODAY versus the ramp schedule, and whether any new 1+ GW PPA has been signed since early May.

## DIFF vs mental model

- **FTM transition is done, not in progress.** I had it as "executing during the spring outage"; the 10-Q states it occurred in April 2026. The FERC/BTM regulatory overhang is fully resolved and the 1,920 MW contract is now operational structure, not pending milestone.
- **Current AI revenue is smaller than the narrative suggests.** Pre-transition AWS load was ~300 MW — roughly 5% of revenue (see D1 math). The directness of TLN's AI exposure today is in the *contract and capacity market*, not the current revenue mix. I had not internalized how early in the ramp we are.
- **Capacity revenue is a bigger, quantified second leg than I appreciated:** $1,067M locked for 2027/28 alone (vs ~$200M/yr run-rate two years ago), and PJM itself attributes the demand driver almost entirely to data centers. This is direct scarcity monetization, though paid by the market rather than a hyperscaler.
- **Cornerstone closing is earlier/more de-risked than "summer 2026" implied** — all approvals in hand as of June 1.
- **Unchanged:** no second hyperscaler deal signed; pipeline remains narrative. The 50% contracted-margin target still requires deals that don't exist yet.

## Evidence for D1 (AI Revenue %)

AWS-tied revenue flowing TODAY is small: pre-April-2026 deliveries were ~300 MW (Power Magazine, 2025-06). Derived math: $1.4B/yr at 1,920 MW implies a blended rate of ~$83/MWh ($1.4B ÷ [1,920 MW × 8,760h] = $83.3/MWh — derived, contract price NOT disclosed). At ~300 MW around-the-clock: 300 MW × 8,760h × ~$83 ≈ ~$220M/yr ≈ ~$55M/quarter ≈ **~5% of Q1 2026 revenue ($1,129M, 10-Q 2026-05-05)**. The 10-Q has no AWS revenue line item or customer concentration disclosure, so this is an estimate, flagged as such. A broader reading of "AI-tied" could include Q1 capacity revenues ($207M, ~18% of Q1 revenue, 10-Q), since PJM attributes the 2027/28 demand growth ~97% to data centers — but that is market-clearing revenue, not hyperscaler-contracted. By 2029 (840–1,200 MW) AWS revenue scales to ~$610–875M/yr (same derived rate), and ~$1.4B/yr at full ramp vs. ~$4.5B current revenue run-rate (Q1 annualized) — i.e., headed toward ~20-30% direct, but it is ~5% today.

## Evidence for D2 (Supply chain position / pricing power)

Strong and quantified. (1) PJM capacity scarcity: TLN cleared 8,745 MW at $333.44/MW-day = ~$1,067M for 2027/28 (TLN PR 2025-12-17); the price hit the FERC cap and would have been ~$530/MW-day uncapped (Utility Dive, 2025-12) — datacenter load (~5,100 MW of the 5,250 MW forecast increase) is the marginal driver. Q1 2026 capacity revenues 4.2x YoY ($207M vs $49M, 10-Q). (2) PPA premium: derived AWS rate ~$83/MWh is above PJM around-the-clock merchant forwards, but a clean premium-vs-merchant spread is not computable from disclosures — flagged. (3) Management stated new CCGT economics are "significantly above" merchant and require bilateral PPAs or PJM RBP awards to underwrite (Q1 2026 call, 2026-05-05) — i.e., new supply doesn't get built without buyers paying premium, which is the scarcity condition persisting. (4) PPL zone spark spreads +$5/MWh since 3/31 (Q1 call), though PPL-to-West Hub basis widening is a near-term realized-price drag management calls temporary.

## Evidence for D5 (Hyperscaler exposure)

Signed, named, disclosed: AWS PPA — 1,920 MW, through 2042 (~17 years), ~$18B total value, ~$1.4B/yr at full quantity, FTM retail-supplier structure operational since April 2026 (10-Q 2026-05-05; TLN PR 2025-06-11; Power Magazine). Ramp: 840–1,200 MW by 2029, full by no later than 2032, acceleration possible. Plus SMR/uprate exploration with AWS (TLN PR 2025-06-11). This is among the largest single signed hyperscaler power contracts in the sector — at full ramp it covers ~77% of Susquehanna's 2.5 GW output. Beyond AWS: zero additional named hyperscalers. Ohio engagement, "several 1+ GW PPA opportunities," and 3–4 GW of datacenter-capable land are management narrative without counterparties (Q1 2026 call). Concentration cuts both ways: deepest single-name hyperscaler contract in the IPP peer group relative to company size, but single-customer dependency until deal #2 signs.
